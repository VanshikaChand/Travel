Meghalaya Culture

Immerse yourself in the highland traditions of Meghalaya—home to the Khasi, Jaintia, and Garo tribes—celebrated through music, matrilineal customs, dances, vibrant festivals, and sustainable crafts.

Traditional Dance & Music

Shad Suk Mynsiem & Wangala

Khasi’s Shad Suk Mynsiem is a graceful dance celebrating womanhood and nature. The Garos perform Wangala or the 100 Drums Festival, a dynamic post-harvest celebration with drumbeats and warrior dances.

Musical Heritage

Folk music features chants, flutes, drums, and traditional wind instruments. Oral storytelling is often embedded in rhythmic beats and songs passed through generations.

Festivals

Shad Suk Mynsiem

Held every spring by the Khasi tribe, this “Dance of Peaceful Hearts” symbolizes gratitude, purity, and fertility through ceremonial dance and music.

Wangala

A Garo harvest festival featuring 100 synchronized drums, ornate costumes, and thanksgiving to the Sun God (Misi Saljong).

Behdeinkhlam

A Jaintia festival seeking divine protection against diseases. It features colorful processions, wooden structures, and competitive games like “Datlawakor”.

Cuisine

Fresh & Smoky Flavors

Meghalaya cuisine uses natural herbs, fermented foods, and minimal spices. Dishes often include pork, chicken, bamboo shoots, and seasonal vegetables.

Popular dishes include Jadoh (rice with meat), Dohneiiong (pork with black sesame), and Tungtap (fermented fish chutney), enjoyed with local red rice.

Handicrafts & Art

Bamboo Weaving

From rain shields (knup) to baskets and fish traps, bamboo weaving is essential in rural life and is skillfully passed down generations.

Black Pottery

Traditional clay pottery from the Jaintia Hills, made without a wheel and known for its deep black sheen and durability.

Wood & Cane Art

Used in home décor and ceremonial spaces, carvings often reflect tribal legends, animals, and the Khasi-Jaintia cosmology.

Travel Tips for Meghalaya

Explore the abode of clouds with ease—here’s what you should know before your journey.

Best Time to Visit

The best time to visit Meghalaya is from October to April. During this period, the weather is cool and perfect for exploring caves, waterfalls, and trekking trails. Monsoons (June to September) are beautiful but may bring travel challenges due to heavy rains.

Getting Around

  • By Shared Taxi: Common and economical in Shillong and between nearby towns.
  • Private Cabs: Best for reaching remote areas like Dawki, Cherrapunji, and Mawlynnong.
  • Walking & Hiking: Essential for exploring living root bridges and village trails.
  • Public Transport: Limited in rural areas—plan trips with local guides or drivers.

Language & Communication

Khasi, Garo, and English are widely spoken. English is commonly used in cities like Shillong. A respectful “Khublei” (Thank You) is appreciated by locals and often returned with a smile.

Food & Water Safety

Local dishes like Jadoh, Dohneiiong, and Tungrymbai are must-tries. Eat at known eateries and drink bottled or purified water. Avoid street food in remote areas unless hygiene is clearly maintained.

Cultural Etiquette

  • Respect local customs, especially in tribal and village communities.
  • Always ask before taking photos of people or inside sacred sites.
  • Modest and comfortable clothing is ideal, especially during village treks or church visits.
  • Avoid littering—Meghalaya is known for its cleanliness and eco-conscious villages like Mawlynnong.

Safety Tips

  • Meghalaya is very safe, but it’s best to avoid remote travel at night.
  • Carry a light raincoat or umbrella—even dry months can have light showers.
  • Mobile signal may be weak in deep valleys—download offline maps.
  • Hire local guides for treks and cave exploration—they enhance safety and experience.
